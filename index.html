How to Read This Tool

This tool is a diagnostic aid: it works out a likely, evidence-backed explanation from the relay's own recorded data and configured logic. It's meant to be checked against the diagram and supporting tabs, not treated as an unquestionable final answer.

Reading the Logic Chain Diagram

The diagram traces the relay's trip logic from left (earliest contributing condition) to right (the final trip command), reconstructed from the relay's settings and the recorded data for this event.

  • Green, filled box — this condition was true / asserted at the moment of the trip.
  • Grey / muted box — this condition was false / not asserted at that moment. It's only shown when it's structurally required to explain the result (e.g. every side of an AND, or every side of a NOT'd condition that failed together) — a false box is never just decoration.
  • Boxes with a thicker accent/red border — these are internal SV logic/timer variables (the relay's own named intermediate signals), rather than a directly measured element. Hover any box for its underlying equation or description.
  • "ALL required (AND)" — every box in that group had to be true (or false, if explaining a negated condition) for the result shown.
  • "ANY of these (OR)" — only one box in that group needed to be true; alternatives that didn't happen are omitted rather than shown as false.
  • NOT / R_TRIG / F_TRIG prefixes inside a box (e.g. "NOT SV19", "R_TRIG CLOSE") mean the condition is being evaluated as negated, or only counts at the instant it just changed state (rising/falling edge), not merely whenever it happens to be in that state.
  • The final red box on the right is the trip/event command itself. A note below the diagram indicates how many other, unrelated trip conditions exist in the same equation but weren't part of this event.

Frequently Asked Questions

How is this different from just opening the file in QuickSet or SynchroWAVe?
QuickSet shows how the relay is configured; SynchroWAVe shows what was recorded. Neither automatically connects the two for a specific event. This tool does that connection automatically — matching the recorded data against the relay's own pickups, timers, and logic for the event in front of you, which otherwise has to be done manually, equation by equation.

Should I act on a diagnostic flag?
Flags are informational, not instructions — they call out something worth a closer look (e.g. a trip driven by internal logic, an already-open breaker, or a likely failed voltage-sensing channel) without telling you what to do about it.

Why do some boxes show a condition that's false?
A false box is only ever shown when it's structurally necessary — e.g. every side of an AND, or a set of conditions that all had to fail together to explain a NOT. It's never shown just for reference.

Using the Tabs

The tabs below the summary give you the underlying evidence behind the headline determination. None of this data is new — it all comes from the same CEV file QuickSet or SynchroWAVe would open — but it's pre-cross-referenced against the relay's own settings and against each other, which normally has to be done by hand.

  • 📍 Event Timeline — every digital bit that changed, in order, with exact timing. Unlike a raw digital plot, each entry is already labeled with what it means, so you can scan the sequence of events without reading relay-word bit names.
  • 🔌 Voltages — pre-fault vs. fault voltage on every phase and terminal, with percent change already computed and color-flagged, plus every voltage protection element's pickup compared directly against the measured value for this event. QuickSet shows you the pickup setting; SynchroWAVe shows you the waveform. This puts the two side by side automatically, for this specific event.
  • 〰️ Currents — the same pre/fault comparison for every current channel, with the percent change flagged so a large step (fault current) or a near-zero change (no real current flowing) stands out immediately.
  • 🛡️ Protection — every overcurrent element's pickup, measured value, and multiple-of-pickup, computed for this event rather than left for you to calculate by hand from the settings sheet.
  • 🩺 Diagnostics — physical consistency checks that cross-reference sequence voltage against sequence current (e.g. zero-sequence voltage vs. zero-sequence current). A real, sustained sequence voltage should show up roughly proportionally in the matching current; a large sustained gap points to a CT wiring issue, a grounding transformer problem, or an open phase — a check neither QuickSet nor SynchroWAVe performs on its own.
  • 🔗 SV Logic — every internal SV (timer/logic variable) used in the trip equation, with its pickup/dropout delay and equation shown directly, split out from every other active SV in the relay. This is the same information buried in the settings file, surfaced specifically for the SVs that mattered to this event.
  • 📊 Frequency — frequency protection elements' pickups and measured values for this event, in the same pickup-vs-measured format as the other protection tabs.
  • 📋 Equations — the raw SELogic text for the trip, unconditional trip, fault, and reclose-initiate equations, for anyone who wants to verify the tool's resolution against the relay's own settings directly.

In short: QuickSet tells you how the relay is configured; SynchroWAVe shows you what was recorded. This tool's tabs exist to connect those two — showing, for this specific event, exactly how the recorded data relates to the relay's own configured pickups, timers, and logic — which is the step that otherwise has to be done manually, equation by equation.

  1. Start with the headline cause at the top of the page — this reflects the tool's resolution of the relay's own logic, not a raw relay-word name, though it's still worth confirming against the diagram below it.
  2. Check the diagram to see the actual path, including any supervisory (AND) conditions that had to be satisfied alongside the main cause.
  3. Read any diagnostic flags below the diagram. These call out cases that are easy to misread at a glance — e.g. a trip driven by internal logic rather than a measured fault, a trip issued while the breaker was already open, or a voltage/current mismatch suggesting a failed voltage-sensing channel rather than a real fault. Flags are informational; they intentionally don't tell you what corrective action to take.
  4. Use the charts to confirm the story: current, voltage, and the most relevant derived quantity are synchronized with the digital logic timeline.
