How to Read This Tool

This tool is a diagnostic aid: it works out a likely, evidence-backed explanation from the relay's own recorded data and configured logic. Check it against the logic diagram and the supporting tabs — don't treat it as an unquestionable final answer.

Reading an event, in order

  1. Start with the headline cause at the top of the page — this is the tool's resolution of the relay's own logic for this event, not just a raw relay-word name. It's still worth confirming against the diagram below it.
  2. Check the logic diagram to see the actual trip path, including any supervisory (AND) conditions that had to be satisfied alongside the main cause. (See the Logic Diagram tab of this help for how to read it.)
  3. Read any diagnostic flags below the diagram. These call out cases that are easy to misread at a glance — a trip driven by internal logic rather than a measured fault, a trip issued while the breaker was already open, or a voltage/current mismatch suggesting a failed sensing channel rather than a real fault. Flags are informational; they intentionally don't tell you what corrective action to take.
  4. Confirm the story in the charts. Current, voltage, the most relevant derived quantity, and the digital logic traces are all synchronized on one time axis — the trip and trigger markers land at the same instant on every chart.

Chart controls

  • Scroll over any chart to zoom in or out around your mouse position — all charts zoom together so the traces stay lined up.
  • Click or drag on a chart to place the measurement cursor; it shows exact values on every chart simultaneously at that instant. Drag the cursor line itself to move it.
  • When zoomed, drag empty chart space to pan. Double-click any chart to reset the zoom everywhere.

The diagram traces the relay's trip logic from left (earliest contributing condition) to right (the final trip command), reconstructed from the relay's settings and the recorded data for this event.

  • Green, filled box — this condition was true / asserted at the moment of the trip.
  • Grey / muted box — this condition was false / not asserted at that moment. It's only shown when it's structurally required to explain the result (e.g. every side of an AND, or every side of a NOT'd condition that failed together) — a false box is never just decoration.
  • Boxes with a thicker accent/red border — these are internal SV logic/timer variables (the relay's own named intermediate signals), rather than a directly measured element. Hover any box for its underlying equation or description, or click it for full detail.
  • A vertical bracket with a bold "&" — every box spanned by that bracket had to be true (or false, if explaining a negated condition) together for the result shown — an AND relationship.
  • A vertical bracket with no "&" — these boxes are alternatives that converge into one line; only one needed to be true — an OR relationship. Alternatives that didn't happen are omitted rather than shown as false.
  • NOT / R_TRIG / F_TRIG prefixes inside a box (e.g. "NOT SV19", "R_TRIG CLOSE") mean the condition is being evaluated as negated, or only counts at the instant it just changed state (rising/falling edge), not merely whenever it happens to be in that state.
  • The final red box on the right is the trip/event command itself. A note below the diagram indicates how many other, unrelated trip conditions exist in the same equation but weren't part of this event.

The tabs below the summary hold the underlying evidence behind the headline determination. None of this data is new — it all comes from the same CEV file QuickSet or SynchroWAVe would open — but it's pre-cross-referenced against the relay's own settings and against each other, which normally has to be done by hand.

  • 📍 Event Timeline — every digital bit that changed, in order, with exact timing. Unlike a raw digital plot, each entry is already labeled with what it means, so you can scan the sequence of events without decoding relay-word bit names.
  • 🔌 Voltages — pre-fault vs. fault voltage on every phase and terminal, with percent change already computed and color-flagged, plus every voltage protection element's pickup compared directly against the measured value for this event.
  • 〰️ Currents — the same pre/fault comparison for every current channel, with the percent change flagged so a large step (fault current) or a near-zero change (no real current flowing) stands out immediately.
  • 🛡️ Protection — every overcurrent element's pickup, measured value, and multiple-of-pickup, computed for this event rather than left for you to calculate by hand from the settings sheet.
  • 🩸 Diagnostics — physical consistency checks that cross-reference sequence voltage against sequence current (e.g. zero-sequence voltage vs. ground current). A real, sustained sequence voltage should show up roughly proportionally in the matching current; a large sustained gap points toward a CT wiring issue, a grounding transformer problem, or an open phase — a check neither QuickSet nor SynchroWAVe performs on its own.
  • 🔗 SV Logic — every internal SV (timer/logic variable) used in the trip equation, with its pickup/dropout delay and equation shown directly, split out from every other active SV in the relay.
  • 📊 Frequency — frequency protection elements' pickups and measured values for this event, in the same pickup-vs-measured format as the other protection tabs.
  • 📋 Equations — the raw SELogic text for the trip, unconditional trip, fault, and reclose-initiate equations, for anyone who wants to verify the tool's resolution against the relay's own settings directly.

Any three-phase voltage or current can be split into three simpler components: positive-, negative-, and zero-sequence. None of these are something you'd read directly off a single wire with a meter — they're calculated from all three phases together, specifically to reveal imbalance that a single-phase reading can't show.

  • Positive-sequence is the "normal" component — three equal magnitudes, 120° apart, in the system's normal phase order. In a healthy, balanced system, essentially all of the voltage/current is positive-sequence; it's what does the actual work.
  • Negative-sequence is a measure of imbalance between the phases. It's zero in a perfectly balanced system, and appears when the three phases become unequal relative to each other — a single-phase fault, an open conductor or blown fuse, or a badly unbalanced load are the classic causes.
  • Zero-sequence is different in kind: a component that's identical in all three phases at once, rising and falling together instead of canceling out. It typically shows up around ground faults — zero-sequence current needs a return path through ground/neutral to flow at all, and zero-sequence voltage (3V0) appears when the system neutral shifts away from ground.

Operationally: a trip on a sequence quantity means the relay saw an imbalance between the phases — that alone doesn't say what caused it. Pair it with the actual phase current and voltage for the event: real, elevated current alongside the sequence signal points toward an actual fault; little to no current change alongside it points toward something else (an instrumentation issue, a disturbance without local current, inverter response, etc.) — the sequence element tells you "unbalanced," the surrounding data tells you the rest. The Diagnostics tab automates exactly this cross-check.

How is this different from just opening the file in QuickSet or SynchroWAVe?
QuickSet shows how the relay is configured; SynchroWAVe shows what was recorded. Neither automatically connects the two for a specific event. This tool does that connection automatically — matching the recorded data against the relay's own pickups, timers, and logic for the event in front of you, which otherwise has to be done manually, equation by equation.

Should I act on a diagnostic flag?
Flags are informational, not instructions — they call out something worth a closer look (e.g. a trip driven by internal logic, an already-open breaker, or a likely failed voltage-sensing channel) without telling you what to do about it.

Can the headline cause be wrong?
It can. The headline is a resolution of the relay's configured logic against the recorded data — if a setting was captured incompletely, or the event spans multiple records, the resolution can miss context. The Equations tab shows the raw SELogic so you can verify the tool's reading against the relay's own settings directly.

Why do some boxes in the diagram show a condition that's false?
A false box is only ever shown when it's structurally necessary — e.g. every side of an AND, or a set of conditions that all had to fail together to explain a NOT. It's never shown just for reference.
